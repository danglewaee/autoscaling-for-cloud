\begin{abstract}
Autoscaling policies in cloud systems are commonly either reactive or driven by point forecasts of future demand. Under bursty workloads, both approaches can be brittle: reactive methods respond too late, while point-forecast policies ignore predictive uncertainty and may underprovision during sudden demand spikes. We propose a calibration-aware autoscaling framework that converts predictive uncertainty into scaling decisions through an explicit cost-reliability objective. Rather than scaling to a fixed quantile, our method uses calibrated predictive intervals to balance provisioning cost, underprovisioning risk, and scaling instability. We evaluate the approach on the public Azure Functions Invocation Trace 2021, aggregated at the application level, against reactive, moving-average, point-forecast, and fixed-quantile baselines. In the current implementation, predictive methods reduce aggregate SLA violations substantially relative to reactive scaling, but burst-only windows remain difficult for all methods, which clarifies where calibration-aware decisions must improve further. Our central claim is that forecast accuracy alone is insufficient for robust autoscaling: calibration quality materially affects downstream scaling decisions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Cloud autoscaling is fundamentally a decision-making problem under uncertainty. In practice, many autoscaling policies are either reactive, triggered by observed threshold violations, or predictive, relying on point forecasts of future workload demand. Both paradigms struggle when workloads are bursty, non-stationary, or exhibit abrupt shifts. Reactive methods often respond too late, while point forecasts conceal uncertainty and encourage brittle scaling decisions.

This paper argues that forecast accuracy alone is not enough for robust autoscaling. What matters is whether predictive uncertainty is sufficiently calibrated to support downstream capacity decisions. A forecast with low point error but poorly calibrated uncertainty can still produce costly or unsafe provisioning outcomes. We therefore study calibration-aware autoscaling: a framework that explicitly integrates predictive uncertainty into the scaling policy through a cost-reliability objective.

\paragraph{Problem statement.}
Given historical application-level invocation traces and a short prediction horizon, can calibrated predictive uncertainty improve autoscaling decisions relative to reactive and point-forecast baselines under bursty serverless workloads?

\paragraph{Thesis.}
Calibration-aware autoscaling can reduce SLA violations and improve the cost-reliability trade-off under bursty serverless workloads, and the quality of uncertainty calibration is itself a key determinant of downstream scaling quality.

Our contributions are threefold. First, we formulate autoscaling as a calibration-sensitive decision problem and make explicit how calibration error translates into downstream SLA error. Second, we instantiate this idea with a lightweight residual-calibrated policy that can be reproduced on a public serverless trace without a large forecasting stack. Third, we provide an empirical analysis that separates aggregate behavior from burst-only behavior, which reveals a decision-layer failure mode that average metrics alone would hide.

\section{Related Work}
Autoscaling in cloud systems has long been studied from both reactive and predictive perspectives. Early predictive systems used workload forecasting and limited-horizon control to provision resources ahead of demand variation \cite{roy2011efficient,gandhi2014adaptive}. More recent work has explored learning-based controllers, including reinforcement learning in production environments \cite{qiu2023aware}. Our work is narrower in scope than these systems papers: rather than proposing a richer controller stack, it asks whether uncertainty calibration itself changes the cost-reliability operating point of autoscaling decisions.

A second thread of work studies predictive calibration and interval construction. Guo et al.\ showed that modern predictors can be poorly calibrated even when they are accurate in the aggregate \cite{guo2017calibration}. Conformal prediction provides a general route to coverage-controlled prediction sets under exchangeability \cite{shafer2008tutorial}, and subsequent work has adapted these ideas to sequential and time-series settings \cite{xu2021conformal}. Our method is motivated by this literature, but it intentionally stops short of a full conformal wrapper: the current goal is to isolate the decision consequences of lightweight residual calibration on a public serverless trace.

The closest conceptual overlap lies in decision-aware use of predictive uncertainty. Much of the autoscaling literature evaluates forecast error, utilization, or controller reward; by contrast, our emphasis is on the translation from calibration error to SLA error, and on the empirical fact that burst-only windows reveal failure modes that aggregate metrics can hide. This is also why the paper studies an exploratory hybrid guard: the current evidence suggests that the main unresolved difficulty lies in the decision layer, not only in the forecaster.

\section{Problem Formulation and Theoretical Motivation}
Let $H_t$ denote the observed application-level workload history up to time $t$, and let $D_{t+h} \in \mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}$ denote the random required capacity at forecast horizon $h$. The autoscaling decision is to choose an integer capacity $c_t \in \mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}$ before $D_{t+h}$ is observed.

\begin{definition}[Autoscaling policy and violation rate]
An autoscaling policy is a measurable map $\pi$ from histories to capacities, $c_t=\pi(H_t)$. Its one-step underprovision event is $\{D_{t+h}>c_t\}$, and its violation rate is
\[
V(\pi)=\mathbb{P}(D_{t+h}>c_t).
\]
This is the probabilistic object behind the empirical SLA violation rate reported in the experiments.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Upper calibration and calibration gap]
For a nominal tail level $\alpha \in (0,1)$, an upper predictive bound $U_{1-\alpha}(H_t)$ is said to be \emph{$\varepsilon$-upper-calibrated} if
\[
\mathbb{P}\!\bigl(D_{t+h} \le U_{1-\alpha}(H_t)\bigr) \ge 1-\alpha-\varepsilon.
\]
The ideal case is $\varepsilon=0$. The quantity $\varepsilon$ is the population analogue of the empirical calibration gap used in the experiments.
\end{definition}

\begin{assumption}[Residual transferability within an application]
For a fixed application and forecast horizon, the residual distribution estimated on the calibration split is representative of the residual distribution encountered at decision time. This is the population-level assumption behind the residual-based interval construction used in the current implementation.
\end{assumption}

The paper treats autoscaling as a decision problem under predictive uncertainty. A forecasting module produces either a predictive distribution or a point forecast with an uncertainty estimate for $D_{t+h}$. The decision layer then solves
\[
\mathcal{L}_t(c_t) =
\lambda_c \, c_t
\;+\;
\lambda_u \, \mathbb{E}\!\left[(D_{t+h} - c_t)_+\right]
\;+\;
\lambda_s \, |c_t - c_{t-1}|,
\]
where $(x)_+ = \max(x,0)$, $\lambda_c > 0$ is the unit provisioning cost, $\lambda_u > 0$ is the underprovision penalty weight, and $\lambda_s > 0$ penalizes oscillatory scaling.

This objective is intentionally simple. It is rich enough to connect predictive uncertainty to downstream decisions, while remaining interpretable and easy to evaluate on a public trace.

\begin{theorem}[Quantile rule in the static case]
\label{thm:quantile-rule}
Consider the special case $\lambda_s = 0$ and assume the demand distribution for $D_{t+h}$ is known exactly. Then any optimizer of
\[
\lambda_c \, c + \lambda_u \, \mathbb{E}\!\left[(D_{t+h} - c)_+\right]
\]
corresponds to a quantile decision rule. In particular, for the continuous idealization, the optimal capacity is an $\alpha$-quantile of $D_{t+h}$ with
\[
\alpha = 1 - \frac{\lambda_c}{\lambda_u},
\qquad \text{provided } 0 < \lambda_c < \lambda_u.
\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The derivative of the objective with respect to $c$ is
\[
\frac{d}{dc}\mathcal{L}(c)
=
\lambda_c - \lambda_u \, \mathbb{P}(D_{t+h} > c).
\]
At optimality, the tail probability balances marginal cost and marginal underprovision penalty, so the solution is a quantile of the demand distribution. This shows that fixed-quantile scaling is not arbitrary: it is the optimizer of a simplified asymmetric-cost decision problem.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:quantile-rule} gives a clean theoretical interpretation of the fixed-quantile baseline and also clarifies the proposed method: calibration-aware scaling is a generalization of this quantile logic to a setting with uncertainty estimation errors and a temporal smoothness penalty.

\begin{theorem}[Calibration transfers directly to SLA control]
\label{thm:calibration-to-sla}
Let $U_{1-\alpha}(H_t)$ be an $\varepsilon$-upper-calibrated predictive upper bound for $D_{t+h}$, and define the capacity decision
\[
c_t = \left\lceil U_{1-\alpha}(H_t) \right\rceil.
\]
Then the induced policy $\pi$ satisfies
\[
\mathbb{P}(D_{t+h} > c_t) \le \alpha + \varepsilon.
\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Because $c_t \ge U_{1-\alpha}(H_t)$ by construction,
\[
\{D_{t+h} > c_t\} \subseteq \{D_{t+h} > U_{1-\alpha}(H_t)\}.
\]
Taking probabilities and using $\varepsilon$-upper-calibration gives
\[
\mathbb{P}(D_{t+h} > c_t)
\le
1 - \mathbb{P}\!\bigl(D_{t+h} \le U_{1-\alpha}(H_t)\bigr)
\le
\alpha + \varepsilon.
\]
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:calibration-to-sla} is the core decision-theoretic reason the paper cares about calibration. A nominal ``P95'' scaling rule only deserves that interpretation if the upper bound is close to a true 95th percentile under the deployed data distribution.

\begin{proposition}[Miscalibration produces excess violations]
\label{prop:miscalibration}
Suppose a policy is intended to operate at tail level $\alpha$, but its realized exceedance probability is $\alpha+\delta$ for some $\delta>0$. Then the policy incurs an additive excess violation rate of $\delta$ relative to its design target. In particular, any downstream SLA-oriented evaluation that depends on the indicator $\mathbf{1}\{D_{t+h}>c_t\}$ inherits that additive degradation directly.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
This is immediate from the identity $\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}\{D_{t+h}>c_t\}]=\mathbb{P}(D_{t+h}>c_t)=\alpha+\delta$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Conditional calibration matters]
Global calibration averaged over applications does not imply calibration conditional on a specific application. Since scaling decisions in this paper are made per application, pooled residual calibration can be misleading even when aggregate coverage looks acceptable. This is the theoretical motivation for the app-specific calibration ablation studied later in the paper.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Burst-guard envelope guarantee]
\label{prop:burst-guard}
Consider a hybrid policy of the form
\[
c_t = \max\!\left\{\tilde c_t,\left\lceil \beta \max_{1 \le j \le L} D_{t-j} + b \right\rceil\right\},
\]
where $\tilde c_t$ is any base calibration-aware decision, $\beta \ge 1$ is a multiplicative guard factor, $L \ge 1$ is a look-back window, and $b \ge 0$ is a buffer. If the realized next-step demand satisfies
\[
D_t \le \beta \max_{1 \le j \le L} D_{t-j} + b,
\]
then the policy cannot underprovision at time $t$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
By construction,
\[
c_t \ge \left\lceil \beta \max_{1 \le j \le L} D_{t-j} + b \right\rceil \ge D_t.
\]
Hence $D_t > c_t$ is impossible.
\end{proof}

This proposition is deliberately simple, but it captures the role of the exploratory burst-aware guard introduced in the empirical section: once a burst is partially revealed, a short reactive envelope can protect subsequent minutes of the same burst, at the price of higher oscillation and transient overprovisioning.

\begin{remark}[Role of the oscillation penalty]
When $\lambda_s > 0$, the policy no longer reduces to a static quantile rule. The optimal decision becomes history dependent through $c_{t-1}$, which creates a direct trade-off between burst responsiveness and scaling stability. This is precisely where a calibration-aware decision policy differs from a naive fixed-quantile baseline.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Scope of the theoretical claims]
The current implementation uses empirical residual quantiles rather than a full conformalized construction, so the paper does not claim an exact finite-sample coverage theorem. The role of the theory is narrower and more honest: it formalizes the decision problem, shows how calibration error translates into SLA error, and explains why app-specific calibration and burst-aware guards are natural extensions.
\end{remark}

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
\subsection{Overview}
The proposed framework has three layers:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.2cm]
    \item \textbf{Forecasting layer:} predicts short-horizon demand and produces predictive uncertainty.
    \item \textbf{Calibration layer:} adjusts uncertainty estimates so that predictive intervals better match empirical coverage.
    \item \textbf{Decision layer:} converts the calibrated demand distribution into a scaling decision using an explicit cost-risk objective.
\end{enumerate}
The implementation is intentionally lightweight so that downstream differences can be attributed mainly to calibration and decision logic rather than to predictor scale.

\subsection{Forecasting Model}
For each application we construct a minute-level required-capacity sequence $\{y_t\}_{t=1}^T$. Given a history window of length $m$ and forecast horizon $h$, the forecasting module predicts
\[
\hat y_{t+h} = f_{\theta}(y_{t-m+1}, \ldots, y_t).
\]
In the current implementation, $f_\theta$ is a linear autoregressive model. This choice is deliberate: it keeps the predictor simple enough that changes in downstream performance can be attributed mainly to calibration and decision logic rather than to model scale.

\subsection{Calibration Layer}
Let $\mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{cal}}$ denote the calibration split and define residuals
\[
r_i = y_i - \hat y_i, \qquad i \in \mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{cal}}.
\]
The method uses the empirical residual distribution in two ways. First, it forms a calibrated interval
\[
\bigl[\ell_{t+h}, u_{t+h}\bigr]
=
\Bigl[
\max(0,\hat y_{t+h}+q_{\alpha/2}(R)),
\max(0,\hat y_{t+h}+q_{1-\alpha/2}(R))
\Bigr],
\]
where $R=\{r_i : i\in\mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{cal}}\}$ and $q_p(R)$ denotes the empirical $p$-quantile of the residual set. Second, it forms a scenario set for downstream decisions,
\[
\mathcal{S}_{t+h} = \{\max(\hat y_{t+h}+r,0): r \in R\}.
\]
The default configuration pools residuals globally. A second ablation replaces $R$ with an application-specific residual set $R_a$ to better align calibration with per-application deployment.

\subsection{Decision Layer}
Given the scenario set $\mathcal{S}_{t+h}$ and previous capacity $c_{t-1}$, the calibration-aware policy chooses
\[
\hat c_t
=
\arg\min_{c \in \mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}}
\left[
\lambda_c c
\;+\;
\lambda_u \frac{1}{|\mathcal{S}_{t+h}|} \sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}_{t+h}} (s-c)_+
\;+\;
\lambda_s |c-c_{t-1}|
\right].
\]
This objective balances provisioning cost, expected underprovision under calibrated demand scenarios, and temporal smoothness. The candidate set is discrete, so the implementation solves the objective by enumeration over a bounded integer range.

\subsection{Exploratory Hybrid Variant}
The main method uses $\hat c_t$ directly. An exploratory variant augments it with a short reactive guard,
\[
c_t^{\mathrm{hyb}} = \max\!\left\{\hat c_t,\left\lceil \beta \max_{1 \le j \le L} y_{t-j} + b \right\rceil\right\},
\]
where $L$ is a short look-back horizon, $\beta$ is a multiplicative guard factor, and $b$ is an additive buffer. Proposition~\ref{prop:burst-guard} explains the theoretical motivation for this construction: it is a decision-layer envelope for partially revealed bursts rather than a separate forecasting model.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\subsection{Dataset and Preprocessing}
The empirical study uses the Azure Functions Invocation Trace 2021 as a public serverless case study. For each invocation, start time is reconstructed from the released end timestamp and duration, after which the trace is aggregated at the application level into 1-minute buckets. The main target is minute-level required capacity derived from average concurrent demand within each minute. The primary setup keeps the top 10 most active applications, uses chronological train/validation/test splits of 70/15/15, and evaluates a 5-minute forecast horizon in the first round.

\subsection{Baselines}
We compare the proposed method against four baselines:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.2cm]
    \item reactive threshold autoscaling,
    \item moving-average forecast with scaling,
    \item linear autoregressive point-forecast scaling,
    \item fixed-quantile scaling at level $0.95$.
\end{enumerate}
These baselines cover the main design families that the paper aims to separate: reactive control, smoothing heuristics, point-forecast-driven control, and quantile-based uncertainty heuristics.

\subsection{Metrics}
Forecast quality is measured by MAE and RMSE, with pinball loss for the fixed-quantile baseline. Calibration quality is measured by empirical coverage, calibration gap, and average interval width. Downstream scaling quality is measured by mean provisioning cost, SLA violation rate, underprovision, overprovision, and oscillation.

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}
The empirical evaluation has four components. First, we compare all methods on the full test split. Second, we isolate burst windows using a rolling-baseline detector and evaluate each policy only on those windows. Third, we study an alternative capacity proxy based on per-minute peak concurrency to test whether a sharper target changes the conclusions. Fourth, we report an exploratory hybrid-guard ablation to understand whether the main bottleneck lies in uncertainty estimation or in the decision layer.

\subsection{Discussion of the First Round}
The first empirical round supports several parts of the project's thesis:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2cm]
    \item reactive autoscaling is not competitive on this trace,
    \item fixed-quantile scaling is not automatically safer than a calibrated decision rule,
    \item burst-only analysis reveals weaknesses that aggregate metrics can hide.
\end{itemize}

At the same time, the current evidence does \emph{not} yet justify a strong claim that the calibration-aware method clearly dominates point-forecast scaling. The next iteration should therefore focus on improving burst-sensitive decision behavior rather than adding a more complicated forecasting model too early.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
This study has several limitations. First, the current calibration layer is residual-based rather than fully conformalized, so the paper argues from population-level calibration logic instead of claiming exact finite-sample validity. Second, the empirical study uses one public serverless trace and a primary average-concurrency proxy, which is enough for a focused first paper but not yet enough for broad generalization claims across cloud settings. Third, the exploratory hybrid guard improves burst-only SLA but increases oscillation, which confirms that burst robustness is still an open decision-layer problem rather than a solved component.

Future work should therefore prioritize method refinement over scope expansion. The most valuable next steps are to make the burst-aware guard more selective, test intermediate capacity proxies between average and peak concurrency, and then revisit stronger forecasters only after the decision layer is stable.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper studies autoscaling as a risk-sensitive decision problem rather than a pure forecasting problem. The theory section shows why calibration error translates directly into SLA error, and the empirical study shows that average-case forecasting accuracy alone is not enough to characterize scaling quality. On the Azure Functions 2021 trace, calibration-aware scaling is competitive on aggregate metrics, while burst-only evaluation reveals that the main remaining bottleneck lies in the decision layer. Taken together, these results support the paper's central claim: predictive uncertainty is useful for autoscaling only when it is calibrated and coupled to an appropriate decision rule.

\section{Appendix: Proof Sketches and Implementation Mapping}
\subsection{Expanded Proof Sketch for Theorem~\ref{thm:quantile-rule}}
Let
\[
J(c) = \lambda_c c + \lambda_u \, \mathbb{E}[(D-c)_+]
\]
in the static case, where $D$ abbreviates $D_{t+h}$. Under a continuous demand distribution with density $f_D$, differentiation under the integral gives
\[
\frac{d}{dc}\mathbb{E}[(D-c)_+] = -\mathbb{P}(D>c).
\]
Hence
\[
J'(c)=\lambda_c-\lambda_u \mathbb{P}(D>c).
\]
Setting $J'(c)=0$ yields $\mathbb{P}(D>c)=\lambda_c/\lambda_u$, or equivalently
\[
\mathbb{P}(D \le c)=1-\frac{\lambda_c}{\lambda_u}.
\]
Thus the optimizer is a demand quantile. The discrete-capacity implementation used in the experiments is the integer analogue of this argument.

\subsection{Expanded Proof Sketch for Theorem~\ref{thm:calibration-to-sla}}
The theorem is a one-line decision consequence of upper calibration, but it is worth making the logic explicit. If $U_{1-\alpha}(H_t)$ is a valid upper bound, then choosing capacity at or above that bound ensures that any SLA violation must also be a failure of the upper bound itself. The deployed policy therefore inherits the same tail error rate, up to the calibration gap $\varepsilon$. This is precisely why the paper treats calibration metrics as decision-relevant rather than merely descriptive.

\subsection{Implementation Mapping}
The implementation corresponding to the main method in the paper has four steps:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.2cm]
    \item fit a linear autoregressive model on the training split,
    \item compute residuals on the validation split,
    \item construct a residual-based interval and scenario set for each decision time,
    \item enumerate candidate capacities and choose the one minimizing the objective in Section~\ref{sec:method}.
\end{enumerate}
In the current experiments, the main setting uses a 30-minute history window, a 5-minute forecast horizon, nominal coverage $0.90$, and decision weights $(\lambda_c,\lambda_u,\lambda_s)=(1,8,0.25)$. These values should be read as the first stable operating point of the pipeline rather than as tuned final hyperparameters.
